# Editor's Canon — Dancing with the Stars

## 1. The Premiere

tag: The American debut — the format proved itself in six episodes.
slot_argument: Season one established the format, the tone, and the casting frame every later season has worked inside. The six-episode run is short enough that the show never has time to lose the room — it earns the top slot on merit.
community_rank_hint: rank=1 delta=0 sentiment=hold

The first season of Dancing with the Stars ran six episodes in the summer of 2005 and settled the question of whether the format could cross the Atlantic in about forty minutes. Celebrity-professional pairings, a panel of three judges, live phone votes, weekly eliminations — the structure was borrowed from the BBC, but the casting energy was entirely its own. Production found its rhythm before the second episode and held it through a run short enough that the format's weaknesses never had time to accumulate. The canon places it first because the format it introduced has run without a structural overhaul across every season since.

## 5. Peak Ratings

tag: The format at maximum cultural reach — the fall 2007 audience was the largest the show produced.
slot_argument: Season five earns second place as the show's peak cultural moment. Record viewership and real competitive quality in the same run is rare enough to place it near the top of any canon that takes the format's wider influence seriously.
community_rank_hint: rank=2 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season five is the ratings high-water mark for the series. The fall 2007 run drew the largest audience the format had produced, and the eleven-episode competition held together well enough to justify the attention. That combination — format quality meeting maximum cultural reach — is rare enough to place the season second in the canon. The casting brought the energy the moment required, and the week-to-week elimination arc delivered genuine competitive tension rather than simply filling time between spectacle numbers. As a document of what the show was capable of at its widest, it belongs here.

## 3. Fall 2006

tag: The first long run — twelve episodes and a higher celebrity profile.
slot_argument: Season three earns third place for extending to twelve episodes and raising the celebrity casting bar — the season that proved the format could sustain a longer arc and confirmed DWTS had cleared the novelty threshold.
community_rank_hint: rank=3 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season three moved to fall and stretched to twelve episodes, the longest competitive run the show had attempted. The celebrity lineup carried more profile than either of the first two seasons, signaling that the show had crossed from novelty into must-do television for talent agents. Longer elimination arcs tend to reveal the format's strengths or expose its weaknesses; this run did the former. The back-half episodes developed real competitive stakes as the field narrowed, and production's confidence in the extended format was visible in every production element. It reads today as the season the format grew up.

## 4. The Pro Debut

tag: New professional talent that would define the show for years — the season the pro bench deepened.
slot_argument: Season four earns fourth place through the longer lens: the debut of professional talent who defined the show's competitive identity for years carries outsized historical weight relative to its episode count.
community_rank_hint: rank=4 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season four ran ten spring 2007 episodes and introduced professional dancers who would become central figures in the show's competitive identity through the following decade. Viewed in isolation, it delivered the established format reliably — celebrity arcs, Latin and ballroom disciplines, live-vote drama — without a single defining structural contribution. Viewed across the whole run, it is the season where the pro roster expanded in ways that mattered. Casting that recurs is casting that defines a show. Season four did that quietly, and the canon credits it for a contribution that only became obvious later.

## 2. The First Full Run

tag: The season that established the format cadence every later season followed.
slot_argument: Season two earns fifth place for structural contribution: the two-night weekly cadence, the co-host role, and the ten-episode run format were all decisions made here that every subsequent season inherited.
community_rank_hint: rank=5 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season two is where the show became itself. The expansion from six episodes to ten, the introduction of Samantha Harris as co-host, and the establishment of the performance-then-results two-night-per-week cadence were all decisions made in spring 2006 that every subsequent season inherited. The celebrity-professional pairs, the panel, the live vote — the bones were all there in season one. Season two wrapped connective tissue around them and built a format that could run across years without structural revision. That contribution earns fifth place ahead of the Olympic-cast season, which executed the format well without changing it.

## 6. The Olympic Cast

tag: Athletic casting that raised the choreographic floor from episode one.
slot_argument: Season six takes sixth place for a fresh casting angle — Olympic competitors raised the choreographic floor in early episodes — executed cleanly without altering the competitive structure that was already working.
community_rank_hint: rank=6 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season six leaned into Olympic casting and got a real payoff from it. Athletes who had spent careers developing body awareness and physical discipline arrived in the ballroom ahead of where entertainment-world celebrities typically start, and the choreography in the opening episodes reflected that. The contrast between trained athletes and conventional celebrity cast members gave the season a clear competitive through-line. As a format experiment, it was subtle — the structure did not change, but the casting input changed what the structure could produce. It sits at the base of the early canon as a season that used the format well.
