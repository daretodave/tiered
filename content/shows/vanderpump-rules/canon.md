# Editor's Canon — Vanderpump Rules

## 1. Season 1

tag: The founding season that set SUR's floor plan as real stakes — before the franchise existed.
slot_argument: A young waitstaff under Lisa Vanderpump treats SUR's actual chain of command as real stakes from the first episode, launching a format that outlasted nearly every other Bravo spinoff.
community_rank_hint: rank=1 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 1 opens with total confidence in its premise: a young waitstaff working under Lisa Vanderpump at SUR, her West Hollywood restaurant, introduced through the Real Housewives of Beverly Hills universe. What makes the debut season work isn't the spinoff pedigree — it's how fast the show treats SUR's actual chain of command as real stakes, not just backdrop. Promotions and Vanderpump's own standards for her staff carry as much weight as any personal storyline, and the ensemble reads like actual coworkers rather than a cast assembled for cameras. Ten seasons of expansion since haven't replaced what Season 1 nailed first, which is why it still tops this canon.

## 6. Season 6

tag: The show's biggest expansion yet — zero departures, its longest episode order to date.
slot_argument: Brittany Cartwright and Lala Kent move up to regulars, Billie Lee and Raquel Leviss join recurring, and twenty-five episodes give the largest ensemble yet room to work without losing anyone.
community_rank_hint: rank=2 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 6 ranks second because it's the clearest proof this format scales. Brittany Cartwright and Lala Kent move up to full series regulars, and Billie Lee and Raquel Leviss join in recurring roles, growing the ensemble to its largest size yet without a single departure to manage. Twenty-five episodes, the longest order the show has run, give that bigger cast real room to work through SUR's chain of command instead of just introducing new faces. Nothing about the restaurant's actual hierarchy gets diluted by the growth, which is rarer than it sounds once a cast gets this big. Confident, uncomplicated expansion.

## 3. Season 3

tag: The first season to prove the format could grow — no departures, its longest order to that point.
slot_argument: Ariana Madix and Tom Schwartz move up to full regulars and James Kennedy graduates to recurring, and twenty-one episodes give the bigger cast room to settle without losing anyone.
community_rank_hint: rank=3 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 3 ranks third for being the format's first successful test of growth. Ariana Madix and Tom Schwartz move up to full series regulars, and James Kennedy graduates from guest spots to a recurring role, all without a single departure to offset. Twenty-one episodes, the longest order to that point, give the expanded cast time to settle into SUR's daily hierarchy instead of rushing new faces through introductions. The restaurant's chain of command still drives the season as much as any friendship or relationship does, proof the show's premise holds even as the cast around it gets bigger. A confident, necessary expansion.

## 7. Season 7

tag: A modest cast addition — the bigger story is the show's world gaining a second real address.
slot_argument: Beau Clark joins in a recurring role while Tom Tom, a bar co-owned by cast members, opens during the season, expanding the format's workplace stakes beyond SUR alone.
community_rank_hint: rank=4 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 7 ranks fourth on the strength of expanding the show's world without disturbing its cast. Beau Clark joins in a recurring role, a modest addition next to the growth spurts of the two seasons before it, and nobody departs. The real news is off the restaurant floor: Tom Tom, a bar co-owned by several cast members, opens during this run, giving the format a second real workplace to draw stakes from. Twenty-four episodes balance that expansion with SUR's familiar chain of command, and the season never loses the thread that's carried the show from the start. Steady growth, plus a genuine structural addition.

## 5. Season 5

tag: A recovery season — the founding cast reunites and adds one more name.
slot_argument: Stassi Schroeder returns to the main cast after a season away, and Brittany Cartwright joins recurring, restoring the ensemble to full strength without a lengthy reintroduction.
community_rank_hint: rank=5 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 5 ranks fifth as a recovery season that puts the founding cast back together. Stassi Schroeder returns to the main cast after a year away, undoing the show's first real subtraction, while Brittany Cartwright joins in a recurring role and adds one more name to a fast-growing ensemble. Twenty-four episodes give the reunited group room to settle back into SUR's familiar rhythms — the chain of command, the friendships, the relationships — without spending much time on reintroduction. It doesn't have the format-defining novelty of the seasons ranked above it, but restoring a departed regular this cleanly is its own kind of achievement.

## 2. Season 2

tag: A confident, low-key sophomore season — proof the format didn't need a fix.
slot_argument: Ariana Madix joins in a recurring role and James Kennedy makes guest appearances, in a seventeen-episode run that barely changes the recipe Season 1 established.
community_rank_hint: rank=6 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 2 ranks sixth because a confident sophomore season isn't the same as a format-advancing one. Ariana Madix joins the cast in a recurring role, and James Kennedy starts turning up as a guest, early names in what becomes a much bigger ensemble later on. Seventeen episodes, the show's shortest order, don't leave much room for those additions to do more than introduce themselves. What the season does prove is that Season 1 wasn't a fluke — the restaurant's chain of command and the staff's tangled relationships carry the show just as well the second time around, even without much new to say.

## 4. Season 4

tag: The format's first real subtraction, balanced by its biggest round of new names yet.
slot_argument: James Kennedy is promoted to a series regular and Faith Stowers and Lala Kent join recurring, while Stassi Schroeder steps back from her regular spot to guest appearances only.
community_rank_hint: rank=7 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 4 ranks seventh because it's the first season built around a real loss rather than pure growth. James Kennedy is promoted to a full series regular, and Faith Stowers and Lala Kent join in recurring roles, but Stassi Schroeder stepping back from her regular spot after three seasons is the bigger story — the show's first proof that the format survives someone leaving. Twenty-four episodes give the reshuffled lineup plenty of runway, and SUR's hierarchy still drives the season even as the faces around it shift. It holds up well for a season managing that much change at once, but the subtraction keeps it out of the top tier.

## 8. Season 8

tag: The show's biggest single-season cast addition, followed within weeks by real news that reshaped it.
slot_argument: Beau Clark is promoted to regular and Max Boyens, Brett Caprioni, and Dayna Kathan join as new regulars, before Bravo's post-season departures recast the show's near future.
community_rank_hint: rank=8 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 8 ranks eighth because its cast growth is genuinely significant, but the ground shifted under it almost immediately. Beau Clark is promoted to a full regular, and Max Boyens, Brett Caprioni, and Dayna Kathan join as new series regulars, the biggest single-season addition the show had made to that point. Twenty-four episodes carry that bigger ensemble through SUR's usual mix of hierarchy and personal stakes competently. But weeks after the season wrapped, Bravo announced Kristen Doute and Stassi Schroeder would leave the show, news that recast the season's cast additions as a prelude to a much bigger overhaul rather than a settled expansion.

## 10. Season 10

tag: A stable, returning cast overtaken mid-run by the show's most-covered real-world story.
slot_argument: Nearly the full prior cast returns, with Ally Lewber joining recurring, before Tom Sandoval's affair with Raquel Leviss became public in March 2023, widely nicknamed Scandoval.
community_rank_hint: rank=9 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 10 ranks ninth because on-screen continuity and off-screen disruption pull in opposite directions here. Nearly the entire prior cast returns — Charli Burnett steps back to a recurring role, Brock Davies appears less often — and Ally Lewber joins in a recurring spot, the kind of stable, low-turnover lineup this canon usually rewards. But the season was already airing when its defining story broke: Tom Sandoval's affair with Raquel Leviss became public in March 2023, a tabloid event the press nicknamed Scandoval almost overnight. That real-world story becomes bigger than the season built around it, which is exactly the kind of disruption this canon ranks below quieter, more settled runs.

## 11. Season 11

tag: The leanest ensemble in years, made up almost entirely of the show's longest-tenured names.
slot_argument: Raquel Leviss does not return, and Lisa Vanderpump, Tom Schwartz, Tom Sandoval, Ariana Madix, Katie Maloney, Scheana Shay, Lala Kent, James Kennedy, and Ally Lewber carry the season instead.
community_rank_hint: rank=10 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 11 ranks tenth as the season that follows the prior year's headlines rather than the one that generated them. Raquel Leviss does not return to the cast, and the season carries on with a noticeably smaller, more veteran-heavy core — Lisa Vanderpump, Tom Schwartz, Tom Sandoval, Ariana Madix, Katie Maloney, Scheana Shay, Lala Kent, James Kennedy, and Ally Lewber, with Brock Davies back in a recurring role. Nineteen episodes work through SUR's familiar hierarchy with fewer moving parts than the seasons around it, which reads as stability after a genuinely disruptive stretch. It's a leaner, calmer season than most of this canon, and that's mostly to its credit.

## 9. Season 9

tag: The most disrupted stretch in the show's run — the cast rebuilt before filming even began.
slot_argument: The longest gap in the show's history follows Jax Taylor and Brittany Cartwright's Bravo departures, and Brock Davies, Charli Burnett, and Raquel Leviss's promotions rebuild the roster from there.
community_rank_hint: rank=11 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 9 ranks eleventh because almost no other season in this canon absorbs this much change before a single scene airs. The longest gap in the show's history, a pandemic-era production pause, separates it from the season before, and Jax Taylor and Brittany Cartwright were let go by Bravo somewhere in the middle of it, ending their runs as series regulars. Seventeen episodes introduce Brock Davies as a new regular and promote Charli Burnett and Raquel Leviss to full-time status, rebuilding the roster almost from scratch. The restaurant's chain of command is still there underneath it, and most of the ensemble carries over — which is exactly why the even deeper reset one season later ranks below it.

## 12. Season 12

tag: The most complete cast reset in the show's history — one name carried over, ten new regulars in.
slot_argument: Lisa Vanderpump is the only holdover from Season 11; ten new cast members, four with prior guest appearances, rebuild the roster at SUR and TomTom from scratch.
community_rank_hint: rank=12 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season 12 sits last because no season in this canon disrupts continuity more completely. Lisa Vanderpump is the only face carried over from Season 11 — every other regular and recurring cast member is gone, replaced by ten new names, four of whom had smaller appearances earlier in the show's run. The season does return to the format's founding premise, with SUR and TomTom's actual restaurant hierarchy driving the stakes again after seasons of personal drama that drifted from the floor. That's real credit. But this canon weighs accumulated continuity above almost everything else, and a reset this total costs more than a single season's format discipline can recover.
