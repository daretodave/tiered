# Editor's Canon — Selling Sunset

## 1. Season 1

tag: The founding season — Netflix's docusoap introduces its brokerage and its format with total confidence.
slot_argument: Season one sets the format's whole grammar in eight episodes: seven agents, one brokerage, listings and office dynamics running in parallel. The cast's chemistry is immediate, and the show never pads its runtime.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 1, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season one keeps the top slot even against five seasons of expansion since — it nails the format immediately. Seven agents, one high-end brokerage, and a season structure that treats real estate and office dynamics as equally important stories. The Oppenheim Group's internal hierarchy is established fast, and the season never overexplains itself: multi-million-dollar listings function as both plot and set dressing, while the cast's confrontational chemistry surfaces early and often. Eight episodes is a tight runtime, but the show uses every one of them, and the format that nine seasons build on is fully operational from the first episode.

## 4. Season 4

tag: The season that finally pushes the format past pure ensemble drama.
slot_argument: Ten episodes, a celebrity-client listing, and the biggest roster reshuffle yet — Season 4 is where the show starts treating its own format as something to expand, not just repeat.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 2, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 4 is where the show stops treating its format as fixed. Ten episodes instead of eight give the season room to breathe, and a prominent celebrity-client listing raises the stakes on the business side without losing the office tension that's always driven the show. Emma Hernan and Vanessa Villela join a roster that's already crowded, while Romain Bonnet steps back to a recurring role — the biggest single-season reshuffle the show had made up to that point. It doesn't unseat the founding season, but it's the first entry to genuinely expand the format rather than just repeat it, and that ambition earns it S tier.

## 5. Season 5

tag: The season that adds a reunion special and never lets it go.
slot_argument: Chelsea Lazkani joins a roster that keeps growing, but the real addition is structural — a standalone reunion that becomes a permanent part of how the show gets watched.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 3, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 5 adds Chelsea Lazkani to an already large roster, but the real news is structural: a standalone reunion special, hosted by Tan France and released after the main run, gives the cast a second stage to work through a season once it's aired. That addition sticks around for every season that follows, which makes Season 5 a genuine hinge point for the format rather than just another ten-episode run. The season itself keeps the brokerage's office dynamics front and center, and the roster is large enough now that the ensemble carries real weight. It's a confident, format-expanding season that just misses the top tier.

## 2. Season 2

tag: The first season to grow the ensemble instead of just returning it.
slot_argument: Romain Bonnet, Davina Potratz, and new agent Amanza Smith widen the bench without losing anyone from the founding cast — a clean, confident expansion of the format's original grammar.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 4, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 2 is the first season to grow the ensemble rather than just return it. Romain Bonnet and Davina Potratz move up from recurring status, and interior designer Amanza Smith joins outright, giving the brokerage a bigger bench without losing anyone from the founding cast. That's a meaningful test for a format built on close quarters, and the season passes it — the office dynamics stay just as sharp with three more people in the room. It's a clean, confident expansion, but it's also mostly a continuation of what Season 1 already built rather than a reinvention, which keeps it a strong A rather than S tier.

## 9. Season 9

tag: The season that brings the reunion back — and brings a new agent with it.
slot_argument: Sandra Vergara joins the roster, and the standalone reunion returns after Season 8 skipped it, with Tan France back to host again. Real recovery, even if it's restoring ground rather than breaking new.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 5, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 9 brings Sandra Vergara into the Oppenheim Group's roster and restores the standalone reunion special after Season 8's one-season gap, with Tan France back behind the reunion desk. That's the format's biggest structural hook returning right when the canon needed it to, and pairing it with a genuine new-agent addition gives the ensemble fresh friction to work through once the ten-episode main run wraps. It doesn't reinvent anything Season 5 didn't already establish, and a comeback still reads as a comeback rather than a leap forward. But putting the reunion back in place, with new blood in the mix, earns this season a real step up from the one before it.

## 6. Season 6

tag: The season with the biggest roster turnover the show has had.
slot_argument: Three departures and two arrivals reshape the office, and a Palm Springs retreat sends the format outside its usual walls for the first time.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 6, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 6 turns over more of the cast than any season before it — three agents depart, including Christine Quinn, who leaves to launch her own brokerage, while Nicole Young and Bre Tiesi join the roster. That's real business turnover, not just cast shuffling for drama, and it gives the season's office dynamics an authentic edge. The group also leaves the Sunset Strip office behind for a retreat filmed in Palm Springs, its first arc built entirely away from the usual setting. Eleven episodes — the longest run yet — need most of that length to reintroduce a roster this changed, which keeps the season just below the format's steadiest entries.

## 7. Season 7

tag: The season that upgrades the office, not the format.
slot_argument: A bigger, more polished office and a fully returning cast make Season 7 a comfortable, competent stretch — real production upgrade, but a physical one, not a structural swing on the format itself.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 7, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 7 doesn't touch the roster at all — every agent from Season 6 returns, and nobody new joins. What changes is the space: the Oppenheim Group relocates to a new office on the same block, roughly double the size of the old one and dressed up with real design polish. That's a genuine upgrade in production value, and it gives the season's office scenes more room to work with, but it's a physical change rather than a structural one. The reunion special returns as expected, keeping the two-part format intact. Eleven episodes of a familiar cast in a nicer room is a fine season — just not one that pushes the format anywhere new.

## 3. Season 3

tag: The stable-roster season that trusts its cast over a shake-up.
slot_argument: No new agents, no departures — Season 3 leans on the relationships Season 2 built and lets Amanza's first major deal carry the season's business stakes.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 8, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 3 makes no changes at all — the same ten-agent roster from Season 2 returns intact, and no one new joins the brokerage. Without a shake-up to lean on, the season leans harder into the existing relationships, and Amanza's push toward her first major deal gives it a genuine business arc alongside the usual desk tension. That's a fine season on its own terms, but it's also the least eventful of the run so far: nothing here tests the format the way a roster change or a structural addition does. It's a solid, competent middle chapter — exactly what B tier is for.

## 8. Season 8

tag: The season that finally lets the reunion go — for one year, anyway.
slot_argument: Alanna Gold joins the roster, but the bigger story is what's missing: the first season since Season 4 without a standalone reunion special, breaking the two-part structure the show had built since Season 5.
community_rank_hint: { rank: 9, delta: 0, sentiment: hold }

Season 8 adds Alanna Gold to the Oppenheim Group's roster and drops all eleven episodes at once, but its defining move is a subtraction: no standalone reunion special, the first time the show has skipped one since Season 4. That's a real step back from a format hook the canon has treated as a hinge point ever since Season 5 introduced it — the reunion gave every prior season a second stage to work through what aired, and this one doesn't get it. A new agent and a same-day episode drop aren't nothing, but they don't make up for losing the structural piece the show has otherwise relied on since Season 5.
