# Editor's Canon — The Real Housewives of Miami

## 1. Season 1

tag: The founding season that launched Miami's social world — before the cancellation, before the revival.
slot_argument: Six women enter Miami's social scene in a debut confident enough to survive what came next — a three-season run, a cancellation, and an eventual return across two networks. The cast set a tone the franchise would keep circling back to.
community_rank_hint: rank=1 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season one earns its spot because it's the only version of RHOM that didn't yet know what the franchise would become. Marysol Patton, Alexia Echevarria, Adriana de Moura, Cristy Rice, Larsa Pippen, and Lea Black introduce a Miami social world built on glamour, family businesses, and old friendships tested by new money — sunnier and more overtly aspirational than most Housewives debuts. Nothing here is hedged against a future cancellation or a Peacock relaunch; the cast plays it straight, and that unguarded quality is hard to replicate once a show knows its own reputation. Every season since has had to answer to this one's confidence.

## 4. Season 4

tag: The Peacock revival that ended an eight-year gap with an almost entirely new cast.
slot_argument: Season four is RHOM's second-biggest milestone — it revives a cancelled franchise on a new network with a cast that owes almost nothing to the version before it. Fresh chemistry and real relaunch stakes earn it the runner-up slot.
community_rank_hint: rank=2 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season four matters because of what it had to prove: that Miami's social world could survive eight years off the air and a nearly total cast reset. Only Lisa Hochstein and Alexia Nepola carry over with full-time history; Larsa Pippen, Guerdy Abraira, Julia Lemigova, and Nicole Martin arrive with no prior seasons to lean on, and the show is better for it. The new cast has to build its own social geometry from scratch, and it does so with real urgency. Peacock's exclusivity gives the format a cleaner canvas than Bravo's later seasons manage, and the relaunch's stakes are visible in every episode. Second in the canon, behind only the original debut.

## 2. Season 2

tag: The original run's first cast overhaul, and one of its strongest ensembles.
slot_argument: Season two proves the original cast can survive turnover — two exits, one demotion, three strong new full-timers reshuffle the group without losing its footing. A solid mid-canon slot behind the two franchise-milestone seasons.
community_rank_hint: rank=3 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season two is the original run's most consequential season for cast alone. Larsa Pippen and Cristy Rice exit, Alexia Nepola steps back to a recurring role, and three new full-time housewives — Joanna Krupa, Lisa Hochstein, and Ana Quincoces — join Lea Black, Adriana de Moura, and Marysol Patton, with Karent Sierra rounding out the group. The turnover could have destabilized the show. Instead it sharpens it: the new additions bring distinct personalities that give Miami's social world more competing energy than the debut had room for. Eighteen episodes let the new configuration breathe. Not a franchise milestone the way the founding or revival seasons are, but a genuinely strong ensemble on its own terms.

## 6. Season 6

tag: The revival cast graduates to Bravo's linear schedule without losing its footing.
slot_argument: Season six is the moment the revival stops being a Peacock experiment and becomes a fully reinstated Bravo show, airing linear with a same-day Peacock stream — a dual-network model still running two seasons later.
community_rank_hint: rank=4 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season six is the revival era's structural turning point. After two seasons exclusively on Peacock, RHOM returns to Bravo's linear schedule while keeping Peacock as a same-day streaming outlet — a hybrid distribution model the franchise hadn't tried before and has kept running since. The cast carries over largely intact from the prior two seasons, which means the social dynamics arrive with real accumulated history instead of starting cold. Ana Quincoces, an original-run housewife, reappears in a guest role, threading the show's two eras together for the first time on screen. Twenty episodes give the format room to run at full length. A confident, structurally important season, ranked just behind the strongest ensemble years.

## 7. Season 7

tag: The most recent completed season adds one new voice to a cast that already runs smoothly.
slot_argument: Season seven takes the dual-network model season six established and runs it with a settled cast plus one deliberate new addition — confident, competent television, just without the historic weight of the seasons that opened new chapters.
community_rank_hint: rank=5 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season seven is the most recent complete chapter of RHOM, and it plays like a show that has fully settled into its current era. Stephanie Shojaee joins as the season's one new full-time housewife, alongside Alexia Nepola, Larsa Pippen, Lisa Hochstein, Guerdy Abraira, and Julia Lemigova; Nicole Martin steps back to a guest role after four seasons as a main cast member. The Bravo-and-Peacock release model season six introduced is now fully routine, which lets the season focus its energy on the cast rather than the format. Twenty episodes run with the confidence of a show that knows exactly what it is. Solid, capable television — ranked just below the seasons that changed the show's trajectory.

## 5. Season 5

tag: A capable, quieter season — same revival cast, less cultural noise around it.
slot_argument: Season five keeps the revival cast intact for a second run, its longest episode count yet. It ranks below the other revival-era seasons as the franchise's commercially quietest stretch, without a milestone or hook to distinguish it.
community_rank_hint: rank=6 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season five is the revival era holding steady rather than pushing forward. The same six main housewives from season four return, joined again by the same recurring cast and a guest appearance from Lea Black, and the longer nineteen-episode order gives the group room to deepen relationships that were still new the season before. That continuity is also the season's limitation: there's no cast overhaul, no network switch, no obvious hook beyond watching an already-established group keep going. It's reportedly the quietest season in the franchise's history by viewership, and the ranking reflects that lack of a defining edge rather than any drop in craft. Competent, familiar television — a comfortable middle slot in a canon full of bigger swings.

## 3. Season 3

tag: A leaner cast closes out the original run, unaware it's about to become an eight-year gap.
slot_argument: Season three shrinks the recurring roster right as the original Bravo era ends, and that contraction shows on screen. It ranks last not as a knock on the cast but because the show was visibly low on runway before the cancellation ahead.
community_rank_hint: rank=7 delta=0 sentiment=hold

Season three closes out RHOM's original Bravo run, and the contraction that preceded the cancellation is visible in the cast list. Alexia Nepola returns to full-time status, but Ana Quincoces and Marysol Patton slide to recurring roles and Karent Sierra appears only as a guest, leaving a thinner five-person core to carry sixteen episodes. The show still functions — Lea Black, Adriana de Moura, Lisa Hochstein, and Joanna Krupa hold the center — but the energy of a full ensemble is missing, and the season reads, in hindsight, like a show running low on momentum right before an eight-year hiatus. Historically significant as the closing chapter of an era; the thinnest cast of the seven seasons ranked here.
